Analog tech, and crafting collages, are back in a big way this year.

2026’s trend, positioned as [a cure for brainrot](https://uxdesign.cc/are-we-creating-brain-rot-dad9e947ba5c), is a return to analog: ==analog technologies like CD and MP3 players,== analog experiences like reading a physical magazine, so-called analog mindsets focused on slowness and intentionality.

People are (ironically) [posting about](https://youtu.be/iz1LqmnGL64?si=fYZsKxlY0Wwfp8a9) how they are trying to log off more often, using reality to escape the internet. They’re carrying crafts and paperbacks rather than relying on their phone to alleviate boredom while waiting on the bus.

Does this mean digital and online experiences are doomed?

Of course not. But this mindset shift does perhaps signal a need for change within the design of those experiences.

## What is the 2026 analog trend?

![An Instagram feed of videos declaring 2026 to be “the year we go analog.”](https://miro.medium.com/v2/resize:fit:640/format:webp/1*0XH690EjMkcZpdbWM03miA.png)

Instagram is filled with people pledging to go analog this year.

[According to The Financial Times](https://www.ft.com/content/a0724dd9-0346-4df3-80f5-d6572c93a863), social media usage peaked globally in 2022 and has actually be on a decline ever since. The online environment has undergone significant changes in the last four years; the proliferation of AI and AI-generated content is just one part of this transformation. Shortform content has taken over and social media is now less about the people…
